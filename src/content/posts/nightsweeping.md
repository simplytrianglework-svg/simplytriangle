## Introduction
Superstitions are something that I’ve heard a lot from the elders of my family. Now that I’m older, I want to investigate the origin of why and how superstitions came to be. For now, let’s focus on a common superstition—never sweep at night unless you want to experience bad luck.

## Folklore Perspective

Let’s focus first on my “claims” and things I know about this superstition. This is the list :

### Known Context
It is commonly known in the Philippines that superstitions are passed down from word of mouth. 

### Verdict
A common interpretation is that the belief discouraged actions that might disturb visiting spirits or creatures. 

### Possible Origin
One possible explanation is that the belief developed as a practical warning. Before electricity became common, sweeping in the dark may have increased the risk of losing small valuables or disturbing objects around the home. 

After gathering my claims and speculations, I want to present to you my research notes.

## Supporting Details

“A common interpretation is that the belief discouraged actions that might disturb visiting spirits or creatures.” 

According to Philippine Folktales : An Introduction, myths are taught to be believed in answer to ignorance, doubt, or disbelief (Bascom, 1965, as cited in Eugenio, 1985). They also stated that myths may recount the activities of deities and spirits that inhabit the world.  Moreover, according to Azeem & Arif (2025), A folklore is unwritten traditions that have been accepted by society. It can be passed down through oral tradition or practices rather than being recorded in writing.  Furthermore, the Pre-colonial religion of the Philippines is animism, a belief that spirits inhabit parts of nature (Reyes, 1985).

## Interpretation

Based on the references, I’ve gathered that since superstitions share characteristics with folklore (Peliño, 2025) ; unwritten traditions that have been accepted by society — we can understand Filipino superstitions as part of the broader tradition of folklore passed down through oral tradition. If we also consider the perspective of Pre-colonial Filipinos that practiced animism, this can answer my following claims : 

“A common interpretation is that the belief discouraged actions that might disturb visiting spirits or creatures.” 

“It is commonly known in the Philippines that superstitions are passed down from word of mouth. “

Continuing, the origin of this specific superstition is something uncertain but looking at the three clues : Oral tradition, spirits activity belief, and animism — It is merely speculation or possible origin that the lack of light in the pre-colonial era may have increased the risk of losing small valuables or disturbing objects around the home hence the bad luck.
